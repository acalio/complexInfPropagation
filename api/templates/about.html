Abstract

To properly capture the complexity of influence propagation phenomena in real-world contexts, such as those related to viral marketing and misinformation spread, information diffusion models should fulfill a number of requirements.
These include accounting for several dynamic aspects in the propagation (e.g., latency, time horizon), dealing with multiple cascades of information that might occur competitively, accounting for the contingencies that lead a user to change her/his adoption of one or alternative information items, and leveraging trust/distrust in the users’ relationships and its effect of influence on the users’ decisions. To the best of our knowledge, no diffusion model unifying all of the above requirements has been developed so far. In this work, we address such a challenge and propose a novel class of diffusion models, inspired by the classic linear threshold model, which are designed to deal with trust-aware, non-competitive as well as competitive time-varying propagation scenarios.
Our theoretical inspection of the proposed models unveils important findings on the relations with existing linear threshold models for which properties are known about whether monotonicity and submodularity hold for the corresponding activation function. We also propose strategies for the selection of the initial spreaders of the propagation process, for both non-competitive and competitive influence propagation tasks, whose goal is to mimic contexts of misinformation spread. Our extensive experimental evaluation, which was conducted on publicly available networks and included comparison with competing methods, provides evidence on the meaningfulness and uniqueness of our models.

Friend-Foe Dynamic Linear Threshold Models
Here we introduce our proposed class of models, which is comprised of: the Non- Competitive F 2 DLT (nC-F 2 DLT ), the Semi-Progressive F 2 DLT (spC-F 2 DLT ), and the Non-Progressive F 2 DLT (npC-F 2 DLT).
All of the models share some commons aspects:
  • Distinction between trusted connections (positive links) and distrusted connection (negative links)
  • Dynamic Behavior via the definition of an Activation-threshold function and a Quiescence function.
    The first one models the tendency of users to increase their belief towards a piece of information which propagates along a network; while the second quantifies the latency in the propagation as function of the number of distrusted neighbors involved in the propagation process.

Semi-Progressive (spC-F 2 DLT)
The second model adheres the scenario where two competing campaigns are spreading simultaneously along the network. We will refer to these campaigns as the “ Good ” and the “ Bad ” campaign.
In this context, a node can be either active in favor of the good campaign or the other.
Also, once a node becomes active it is allowed to switch its activation state from one campaign to the opposite one but it is not allowed to turn back into the inactive state.

Non-Progressive (npC-F 2 DLT)
This model refers to the same competing environment as the previous diffusion model.
However, unlike the Semi-Progressive model, in this case a node is also allowed to turn back to the inactive state if, after its activation, none of the competing campign is still able to maintain its activation, i.e., neither one of the two campaigns is able to exert an amount of influence greater or equal to the node's activation threshold.
